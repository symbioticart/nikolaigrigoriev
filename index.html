Lived time, written daily by an unchanging rule.
Info

Nikolai Grigoriev

I make a life visible so that it can be lived as a work of art. My subject is not death — it is what death sharpens: the attention a life is worth once you know it ends. I take a single life, the quiet traces a body leaves without being asked, and turn it slowly into an image; and each life joins a larger one — many lives gathered into a single form for a museum, a monument to attentive living. The work holds up no live mirror. It takes your life now and never shows it to you now, so there is nothing to perform for and nothing to do with a day but be entirely inside it. Where medicine works to lengthen the body, I work to lengthen a life in the register art can reach — its felt time, and the meaning of having lived it.

I have been afraid of dying since I was a child, and I refused to make that fear my subject. I made its opposite my subject. If death is what ends a life, it is also what makes a life worth composing — the pressure that turns ordinary days into something you would want to have lived on purpose. I aestheticise living, not dying. Death only stands behind me, sharpening the attention.

I take a single life as material — mine first, because I do not ask of a work what I have not lived inside it. A body records itself while you are busy being alive; the work holds up no mirror and asks nothing of you, so what it takes is a life lived, not one staged for it. But no single life is the whole of the work. Each one feeds a larger body: the lives gathered into a piece accrue, together, into a collective form made to stand in a museum — a common portrait of many people living with attention. A private reflection at home; a shared one in public — art at the scale of many lives.

The work takes your life now but never shows it to you now. There is no live mirror to perform for — no way to pose a day, to shape it toward how it will look, to live for the image instead of the moment. I once thought that delay was a flaw. It is the whole point. Emptied of an audience, you are left with the only thing that was ever real: this moment, and the choice to be entirely in it. Being present is not watching your life from a distance; it is disappearing into the day as it happens. What forms later is only the honest residue — the living was the art, and the living is now.

Underneath all of it is a refusal to end. I do not want to die. Where medicine works to lengthen the body, I work to lengthen a life in the register art can reach, and I mean it literally: the work stretches perceived time by slowing it; it keeps every day it has lived, so a life is made imperishable as a record; and it is built to outlast the body, continuing after me from the life of a stranger I will never meet. Three durations against the one that runs out. I came to art late and built its language myself. I do not want to leave objects. I want to leave a way of living, and a work that keeps living it.

Biography

Nikolai Grigoriev (b. 1987, Moscow) is a conceptual artist. He lives and works in Barcelona.

He has been alive for — days. — of them are written into Variations 87.

Each of his works takes a single life — the quiet record a body leaves without being asked — and turns it, day after day, by one unchanging rule, into an image. The work and the life evolve together for as long as the life lasts: the image changes as the life changes, the archive keeps every day, and the rule is made to outlast the body. He came to art late, out of necessity, and intends to leave not objects but a way of living — and works that keep living it.

The work

Variations 87

This is a living work, and its material is lived time itself. Each day of one life is recorded quietly, without performance — its rest and its unrest, the depth of its nights, its warmth, the evenness of its breath — and turned by a single unchanging rule into one painting. The rule never changes; the life does. What hangs here is not the picture of a life but a life made into something that can be held in the eye. Living is the making; nothing is staged.

The work never shows the present. A day can be met only once it has closed, when nothing in it can still be arranged. Because there is no one to live toward and no image to compose while the hours are passing, the day is handed back to the one inside it, to be spent fully and unwatched. The delay is a protection, and the painting is only the honest residue of a day actually lived.

Nothing on the surface is arbitrary, and nothing can be read back out of it. What one life leaves is entangled before it reaches the canvas, so the image is a trace, not an account. The same day always makes the same painting, and once a day has ended its painting never changes: the past stays exactly as it was written. The work does not flatter — a hard day makes a hard image, and that too is a life made visible.

When the life falls silent — by distance, by refusal, by illness, in the end by death — no new day arrives and the last painting sets. Stillness is not the subject of the work but the edge that sharpens it: because the record can stop, each day it keeps is worth this attention. The work carries its own means of survival and may come to rest on its own terms.

One life is only one panel of a larger form. These works are made to gather — many lives, each kept day by day under the same rule, assembled into a single body of days: a monument not to any person but to the act of attentive living. So the meaning is lengthened three ways — the eye is slowed, so a day is felt for longer than it lasted; every day lived is kept and none of it decays, the archive its memory; and the rule is handed on, continuing past the body that began it. I do not lengthen the body. I lengthen the meaning of a life. The form has a lineage — On Kawara, Roman Opałka, Sol LeWitt, Felix Gonzalez-Torres — but here the rule runs on a living body, and one day it will stop.
